Scam Encyclopedia
Every type of crypto scam explained. How they work, how to spot them, and real examples.
2020
Ponzi Schemes: Old Trick, New Blockchain, Same Ending
A Ponzi scheme pays early investors with money from new investors. No actual profit is generated. The math always collapses eventually. Crypto gave Ponzis a new vocabulary: "staking rewards," "algorithmic yields," "liquidity mining." The mechanism is the same.
2021
Pump and Dump: How Insiders Cash Out on Your FOMO
Pump and dump is the simplest scam in crypto. A group of insiders buys a low-cap token cheaply. They coordinate a marketing blitz: social media posts, influencer mentions, Telegram group announcements. The price spikes as retail buyers pile in. Insiders sell at the top. The price crashes. Retail holds the bag.
2022
Phishing: One Click and Your Wallet Is Gone
Phishing is the most effective attack in crypto because it targets the weakest link: the human. No smart contract exploit needed. Just a fake website, a convincing email, or a well-timed DM.
2023
Pig Butchering: The Long Con That Starts With a Text
Pig butchering is the fastest-growing financial scam in the world. The name comes from the Chinese phrase "sha zhu pan," meaning to fatten a pig before slaughter. The scammer builds a relationship, earns trust over weeks or months, then introduces a "great investment opportunity" in crypto.
2023
Fake Airdrops: Free Money That Drains Your Wallet
You open your wallet and find tokens you never bought. They might be worth $50,000 according to the DEX. You try to sell them. The contract requires you to visit a website and "approve" the transaction. That approval gives the contract permission to drain every token in your wallet.
2024
Influencer Scams: The $100K Tweet That Costs You Everything
In 2022, the SEC fined Kim Kardashian $1.26 million for promoting EthereumMax without disclosing she was paid $250,000 to do so. It was a landmark moment, but it barely scratched the surface of influencer-driven crypto fraud.

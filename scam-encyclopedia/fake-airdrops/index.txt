3:T52a,You open your wallet and find tokens you never bought. They might be worth $50,000 according to the DEX. You try to sell them. The contract requires you to visit a website and "approve" the transaction. That approval gives the contract permission to drain every token in your wallet.

Fake airdrops are one of the most common and effective scams in DeFi. Scammers send worthless tokens to thousands of wallets, relying on greed to do the rest. The tokens appear in wallet interfaces and on block explorers. They look real. They are bait.

The technical mechanism is the token approval system. When you interact with a DeFi protocol, you grant it permission to move your tokens. Malicious contracts request unlimited approval. Once granted, the attacker can drain your wallet at any time.

A more sophisticated version: dust attacks. Scammers send tiny amounts of crypto to your wallet. If you interact with those tokens, they can link your wallet to your identity through transaction analysis. Some use the transaction memo field to include phishing links.

The defense is discipline. Never interact with tokens you did not buy. Never visit websites listed in airdrop tokens. Use a separate wallet for airdrops if you participate in legitimate ones. Regularly check and revoke token approvals using tools like Revoke.cash.5:["slug","fake-airdrops","d"]
